  Problems To Treat

  Your current evaluation metric is not paper-equivalent. You are computing sample-level Recall@5, while
  the paper uses mean-class Recall@5. This matters a lot on EPIC-KITCHENS because the class distribution
  is long-tailed. Frequent actions dominate sample-level recall, while mean-class recall gives each
  class equal weight.

  Your validation set is not stable. In your current pipeline, validation is built from participants
  whose frames exist on disk at that moment. That means P01 is evaluated on a different validation
  subset than P20. For a paper, every checkpoint/model must be evaluated on the same fixed validation
  definition.

  Your baseline is not exactly the V-JEPA 2 protocol. The paper uses 32 frames at 8 FPS, 1-second
  anticipation, and an attentive probe designed specifically for anticipation. Your config uses 64
  frames at 60 FPS, which is a much shorter temporal context. That may be valid for your own method, but
  then you must describe it as your protocol, not a V-JEPA 2 reproduction.

  Your probe architecture differs from the paper. The paper uses an attentive probe with final query
  tokens for verb, noun, and action. Your current probe uses a transformer encoder plus mean pooling.
  That is simpler, but it can change results materially.

  Your loss differs. The paper says it uses focal loss for the anticipation probe. You use cross
  entropy. Again, valid as a design choice, but not a strict reproduction.

  Your training is participant-sequential. That is practical for disk limits, but it introduces order
  effects. If you train P01, then P02, then P03, the model sees a curriculum grouped by kitchen/person.
  That is not the same as globally shuffled training over all clips. For a paper, you need either to
  make this a deliberate method or reduce the bias.

  Your action vocabulary handling needs to be aligned with EK100. Make sure the action label space is
  exactly the official train action set, and that validation actions unseen in train are handled exactly
  as the benchmark expects. Do not silently exclude classes unless the official metric does.

  Your baseline result file currently includes failed participants and partial LoRA runs. For paper
  data, keep clean result tables: one run, one protocol, one complete validation set, one metric
  implementation.

  What You Should Do

  First, define the official evaluation protocol and freeze it. Decide whether you are reproducing V-
  JEPA 2 exactly or presenting a modified protocol. If you want paper-like comparison, match the V-JEPA
  2 setup as closely as possible:

  - EK100 train/validation official split.
  - 1-second anticipation.
  - Same frame sampling policy, preferably 32 frames at 8 FPS.
  - Same input resolution as the model variant you compare against.
  - Frozen V-JEPA 2 baseline.
  - Same metric: mean-class Recall@5 for verb, noun, and action.
  - Full validation set evaluation.

  Second, build one clean frozen baseline. Before LoRA, your frozen V-JEPA 2 baseline should be
  credible. If your baseline is far below the paper, LoRA improvements become hard to interpret because
  reviewers will ask whether LoRA helped or whether the baseline was weak.

  Third, decide what your LoRA claim is. Possible paper claims:

  - LoRA improves action anticipation over a frozen V-JEPA 2 probe.
  - LoRA reaches similar performance with fewer trainable parameters.
  - Upper-layer LoRA is enough for anticipation adaptation.
  - Different LoRA ranks trade off performance and memory.
  - QLoRA enables adaptation under tighter GPU memory.

  Each claim needs a matching experiment table.

  Fourth, report trainable parameters and compute/memory. Since your paper is about LoRA, accuracy alone
  is not enough. You should report:

  - Total model parameters.
  - Trainable parameters.
  - GPU memory.
  - Batch size.
  - Training time.
  - Action R@5.
  - Verb R@5.
  - Noun R@5.
  - Mean and maybe standard deviation across seeds if feasible.

  Fifth, fix validation before running all LoRA experiments. Do not compare LoRA variants using your
  current per-participant moving validation subset.

  How To Validate Sequentially With Limited Space

  Yes, you can validate sequentially without storing all validation frames at once.

  The correct idea is:

  1. Finish training a model checkpoint.
  2. Freeze the model for evaluation.
  3. Iterate through validation participants one by one.
  4. For each participant, download/extract only that participant’s validation frames.
  5. Run inference.
  6. Store only predictions and labels, or update metric accumulators.
  7. Delete that participant’s frames.
  8. Move to the next validation participant.
  9. After all validation participants are processed, compute final mean-class Recall@5.

  This gives you full-validation metrics without needing full-validation frames on disk.

  Important detail: do not average the participant scores. That is not the same as full validation mean-
  class recall. Instead, collect predictions and labels across all validation clips, then compute the
  official metric globally.

  For mean-class Recall@5:

  - For each class, compute recall among examples whose ground-truth label is that class.
  - Then average those per-class recalls.
  - Do this separately for verb, noun, and action.
  - Only include classes that have at least one validation example, unless the official EK100 script
    specifies otherwise.

  For storage, you have two good options:

  - Store raw logits or top-5 predictions per validation clip.
  - Store per-class counters: number of samples per class and number of top-5 hits per class.

  The second option is tiny and enough for Recall@5. But storing predictions is better for paper work
  because you can later recompute metrics, debug mistakes, produce confusion analysis, and verify
  results.

  Training Sequentially Is More Complicated

  Sequential validation is fine because evaluation has no gradient updates.

  Sequential training is more sensitive. If you train participant-by-participant, the model may overfit
  recent participants or depend on participant order. For a paper, you should either:

  - Present this as a memory-constrained sequential training method, and test multiple participant
    orders.
  - Or approximate global shuffling by training in smaller rotating chunks.
  - Or precompute/download mini-shards, shuffle within each shard, and cycle through participants
    multiple times.

  If your claim is about LoRA itself, not online/sequential learning, then reviewers will expect a
  training protocol close to standard full-dataset training. If you cannot do that, be explicit: “Due to
  storage constraints, training is performed in a participant-streaming regime.”

  Bottom Line

  Before continuing LoRA experiments, fix these in order:

  1. Full validation set, not current-on-disk participants.
  2. Mean-class Recall@5, not sample-level Recall@5.
  3. Stable, documented frame sampling.
  4. Clean frozen V-JEPA 2 baseline.
  5. Paper-matched probe/loss if you want direct comparison.
  6. Sequential validation by streaming participants and accumulating global metrics.
  7. Clear statement about whether training is standard full-data training or memory-constrained
     participant-streaming.

  Once those are fixed, your LoRA results will be much more defensible.
